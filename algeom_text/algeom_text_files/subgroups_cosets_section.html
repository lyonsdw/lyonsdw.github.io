Section 1.3 Subgroups and cosets

Subsection 1.3.1

Definition 1.3.1. Subgroups and cosets.

A subset \(H\) of a group \(G\) is called a subgroup of \(G\) if \(H\) is itself a group under the group operation of \(G\text{.}\) We write \(H\leq G\) to indicate that \(H\) is a subgroup of \(G\text{.}\) A (left) coset of a subgroup \(H\) of \(G\) is a set of the form

\begin{equation*} gH := \{gh \colon h\in H\}. \end{equation*}

The set of all cosets of \(H\) is denoted \(G/H\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} G/H := \{gH\colon g\in G\} \end{equation*}

Checkpoint 1.3.2.

Consider \(D_4\) as described in Checkpoint 1.1.7.

\begin{equation*} D_4=\{R_0,R_{1/4},R_{1/2},R_{3/4},F_H,F_V,F_D,F_{D'}\} \end{equation*}
  1. Is the subset \(\{R_0,R_{1/4},R_{1/2},R_{3/4}\}\) of rotations a subgroup of \(D_4\text{?}\) Why or why not? Answer
    Yes. The composition of any two rotations is a rotation, and every rotation has an inverse that is also a rotation.
  2. Is the subset \(\{F_H,F_V,F_D,F_{D'}\}\) of reflections a subgroup of \(D_4\text{?}\) Why or why not? Answer
    No. Just observe that \(F_H^2=R_0\) is not a reflection. The group operation on \(D_4\) does not restrict properly to the subset of reflections.

Proposition 1.3.4. Subgroup tests.

Let \(H\) be a subset of a group \(G\text{.}\) The following are equivalent.

  1. \(H\) is a subgroup of \(G\)
  2. (2-step subgroup test) \(ab\) is in \(H\) for every \(a,b\) in \(H\) (\(H\) is closed under the group operation) and \(a^{-1}\) is in \(H\) for every \(a\) in \(H\) (\(H\) is closed under group inversion)
  3. (1-step subgroup test) \(ab^{-1}\) is in \(H\) for every \(a,b\) in \(H\)
Proof.

Definition 1.3.5. Subgroups generated by a set of elements.

Let \(S\) be a subset of a group \(G\text{.}\) We write \(\langle S\rangle\) to denote the smallest subgroup of \(G\) that contains \(S\text{,}\) where smallest means that if \(H\) is a subgroup of \(G\) that contains \(S\text{,}\) then \(H\) contains \(\langle S\rangle\text{.}\) We call \(\langle S\rangle\) the subgroup of \(G\) generated by \(S\text{,}\) and we call the elements of \(S\) the generators of \(\langle S\rangle\text{.}\) [Comment on notational convention: If \(S=\{s_1,s_2,\ldots,s_k\}\) is finite, we write \(\langle s_1,s_2,\ldots,s_k\rangle\) for \(\langle S\rangle\text{,}\) instead of the more cumbersome \(\langle \{s_1,s_2,\ldots,s_k\}\rangle\text{.}\)]

Proposition 1.3.6. Characterization of subgroups generated by a set of elements.

Given a set \(A\text{,}\) a word of length \(k\) written with the elements of \(A\) is a formal string of the form

\begin{equation*} w=a_1a_2a_3\cdots a_k \end{equation*}

where each \(a_i\in A\) for \(1\leq i\leq k\text{.}\) For example, the strings \(aab\text{,}\) \(bab\text{,}\) and \(bbb\) are words of length 3 written with the elements of \(A=\{a,b\}\text{.}\) Let \(S\) be a subset of a group \(G\text{,}\) and let \(T=\{s^{-1}\colon s\in S\}\text{.}\) Prove the following.

\begin{equation*} \langle S\rangle = \bigcap_{H\colon S\subseteq H\leq G}H = \{\text{words written with the elements of} S\cup T\} \end{equation*}
Proof.

Proposition 1.3.7. Cosets as equivalence classes.

Let \(G\) be a group and let \(H\) be a subgroup of \(G\text{.}\) Let \(\sim_H\) be the relation on \(G\) defined by \(x\sim_H y\) if and only if \(x^{-1}y \in H\text{.}\) The relation \(\sim_H\) is an equivalence relation on \(G\text{,}\) and the equivalence classes are the cosests of \(H\text{,}\) that is, we have \(G/\sim_H= G/H\text{.}\)

Proof.

4. Centralizers, Center of a group.

The centralizer of an element \(a\) in a group \(G\text{,}\) denoted \(C(a)\text{,}\) is the set

\begin{equation*} C(a) = \{g\in G\colon ag=ga\}. \end{equation*}

The center of a group \(G\text{,}\) denoted \(Z(G)\text{,}\) is the set

\begin{equation*} Z(G) = \{a\in G\colon ag=ga \;\; \forall g\in G\}. \end{equation*}
  1. Show that the centralizer \(C(a)\) of any element \(a\) in a group \(G\) is a subgroup of \(G\text{.}\)
  2. Show that the center \(Z(G)\) of a group \(G\) is a subgroup of \(G\text{.}\)

7. The order of a group element.
Let \(g\) be an element of a group \(G\text{.}\) The order of \(g\text{,}\) denoted \(|g|\text{,}\) is the smallest positive integer \(n\) such that \(g^n=e\text{,}\) if such an integer exists. If there is no positive integer \(n\) such that \(g^n=e\text{,}\) then \(g\) is said to have infinite order. Show that
\begin{equation*} |g|=|\langle g\rangle|\text{.} \end{equation*}
