Section 1.5 Actions

Subsection 1.5.1

Definition 1.5.1. Group action, orbit, stabilizer.

Let \(G\) be a group and let \(X\) be a set. An action of the group \(G\) on the set \(X\) is a group homomorphism

\begin{equation*} \phi \colon G\to \Perm(X). \end{equation*}

We say that the group \(G\) acts on the set \(X\text{,}\) and we call \(X\) a \(G\)-space. For \(g\in G\) and \(x\in X\text{,}\) we write \(gx\) to denote \((\phi(g))(x)\text{.}\)â1â We write \(\Orb(x)\) to denote the set

\begin{equation*} \Orb(x)=\{gx\colon g\in g\}, \end{equation*}

called the orbit of \(x\text{,}\) and we write \(\Stab(x)\) to denote the set

\begin{equation*} \Stab(x) = \{g\in G\colon gx=x\}, \end{equation*}

called the stabilizer or isotropy subgroupâ2â of \(x\text{.}\) A group action is transitive if there is only one orbit. A group action is faithful if the kernel of the map \(G\to \Perm(X)\) has a trivial kernel.

Beware of potential ambiguity in the notation `\(gx\)'. For example, when \(X\) is a group and \(G\) is a subgroup of \(X\text{,}\) the usual meaning of symbols `\(gx\)' is the product of the group elements \(g,x\) under the group operation. But in the context of an action of \(G\) on \(X\text{,}\) the value of \((\phi(g))(x)\) may be different from the product \(gx\text{.}\) In this case, it is necessary to be explicit and write \((\phi(g))(x)\text{,}\) or use some other notation.
It must be proved that \(\Stab(x)\) is indeed a subgroup of \(G\text{.}\) See CheckpointÂ 1.5.3 below.

Proposition 1.5.4. Orbits of a group action form a partition.

Let group \(G\) act on set \(X\text{.}\) The collection of orbits is a partition of \(X\text{.}\) We write \(X/G\) to denote the set of orbits.

\begin{equation*} X/G=\{\Orb(x)\colon x\in X\} \end{equation*}

Subsection 1.5.2

Theorem 1.5.6. The Orbit-Stabilizer Theorem.

Let \(G\) be a group acting on a set \(X\text{,}\) and let \(x\) be an element of \(X\text{.}\) We have a one-to-one correspondence

\begin{equation*} G/\Stab(x)\leftrightarrow \Orb(x) \end{equation*}

given by

\begin{equation*} g\Stab(x) \leftrightarrow gx. \end{equation*}

Exercises 1.5.3 Exercises

1. Actions of a group on itself.

Let \(G\) be a group. Here are three actions \(G\to \Perm(G)\) of \(G\) on itself. Left multiplication is given by

\begin{equation*} g\to L_g\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(L_g\) is given by \(L_g(h)=gh\text{.}\) Right multiplication is given by

\begin{equation*} g\to R_g\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(R_g\) is given by \(R_g(h)=hg\text{.}\) Conjugation is given by

\begin{equation*} g\to C_g\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(C_g\) is given by \(C_g(h)=ghg^{-1}\text{.}\) Show that each of these maps \(L,R,C\) is indeed a group action.

2. Cosets, revisted.

Let \(H\) be a subgroup of a group \(G\text{,}\) and consider the map

\begin{equation*} R\colon H\to \Perm(G) \end{equation*}

given by \(h\to R_h\text{,}\) where \(R_h(g)=gh\) (this is just the restriction of the right multiplication action to the subgroup \(H\)). Show that the orbits of this action of \(H\) on \(G\) are the same as the cosets of \(H\text{.}\) This shows that the two meanings, potentially different, of \(G/H\) in fact agree. Show that this does not work for the left action of \(H\) on \(G\text{.}\)

3. The natural action of a matrix group on a vector space.

Let \(G\) be a group whose elements are \(n\times n\) matrices with entries in a field \(k\) and with the group operation of matrix multiplication. The natural action \(G\to \Perm(X)\) of \(G\) on the vector space \(X=k^n\) is given by

\begin{equation*} g\to [v\to g\cdot v], \end{equation*}

where the "dot" in the expression \(g\cdot v\) is ordinary multiplication of a matrix times a column vector. Show that this is indeed a group action.
