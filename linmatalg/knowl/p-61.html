A linear map \(\R^1\to\R^1\) has the form \(x\mapsto mx\) for some constant \(m\text{,}\) so the assumption that \(f\colon \R^1\to \R^1\) is differentiable at \(x_0\) in the sense of (4.1) means that there is some constant \(m\) such that
\begin{equation*} \lim_{{h}\to 0} \frac{f({x}_0+ {h}) -f({x}_0)-m{h}}{|{h}|} = 0. \end{equation*}
It follows that
\begin{equation*} \lim_{{h}\to 0^+} \frac{f({x}_0+ {h}) -f({x}_0)-m{h}}{{h}} =0. \end{equation*}
Because the limit \(\lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{mh}{h} = m\) exists, the addition property of limits implies that
\begin{equation*} \lim_{{h}\to 0^+} \frac{f({x}_0+ {h}) -f({x}_0)}{{h}} = m. \end{equation*}
A similar argument establishes that
\begin{equation*} \lim_{{h}\to 0^-} \frac{f({x}_0+ {h}) -f({x}_0)}{{h}} =m. \end{equation*}
The agreement of both the left and right hand limits implies that the two-sided limit exists.
\begin{equation*} \lim_{{h}\to 0} \frac{f({x}_0+ {h}) -f({x}_0)}{{h}} =m \end{equation*}
This is the calculus 1 definition of the differentiability of \(f\) at \(x_0\text{,}\) and further, we have \(f'(x_0)=m\text{.}\)
