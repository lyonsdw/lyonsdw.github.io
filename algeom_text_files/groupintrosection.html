Section 1.1 Examples

Algebra is the study of abstract structure and dynamics. With fundamental and widespread applications in mathematics and science, groups occupy a middle ground between the least and the most complicated algebraic objects. We begin by sketching the idea for the definition.

A mathematical model for one type of interaction dynamic is a function of the form \(f\colon S\times T \to U\text{.}\) A pair \((s,t)\) of objects interact to "create" an object \(f(s,t)\text{.}\) When \(S=T=U\text{,}\) we call a function \(f\colon S\times S \to S\) a binary operation on the set \(S\text{,}\) and we might view such a function as an interaction among objects in set \(S\text{.}\) A group is a set \(G\text{,}\) together with a binary operation \(G\times G \to G\text{.}\) Before we give the details of the required properties of a group's binary operation (see SectionÂ 1.2), the remainder of this section introduces examples of some important and useful groups.

Subsection 1.1.1 Permutations

A permutation of a set \(X\) is a bijection from \(X\) to itself, that is, a function that is both one-to-one and onto. Given two permutations \(\alpha,\beta\) of a set \(X\text{,}\) we write \(\alpha\beta\) to denote the composition of functions \(\alpha\circ\beta\text{.}\)

Definition 1.1.1. Group of permutations of a set.

Let \(X\) be a set and let \(\Perm(X)\) denote the set of all permutations of \(X\text{.}\) The group of permutations of \(X\) is the set \(G=\Perm(X)\) together with the binary operation \(G\times G\to G\) given by function composition, that is, \((\alpha,\beta) \to \alpha\circ\beta\text{.}\) For the special case \(X = \{1,2,\ldots,n\}\) for some integer \(n\geq 1\text{,}\) the group \(\Perm(X)\) is called the symmetric group, and is denoted \(S_n\text{.}\)

Subsection 1.1.2 Symmetries of regular polygons

Informally and intuitively, we say that regular polygons have rotational and mirror symmetries. Specifically, the rotational symmetries are rotations about the center \(O\) of the polygon, clockwise or counterclockwise, by some angle \(\angle POP'\text{,}\) where \(P,P'\) are any two vertices. The mirror symmetries of the polygon are reflections across lines of the form \(\overline{OP}\) or \(\overline{OM}\text{,}\) where \(P\) is any vertex and \(M\) is the midpoint of any edge of the polygon. See FigureÂ 1.1.3.

Here are some standard notations for rotations and reflections in the plane. See FigureÂ 1.1.4.

Rotations in the plane.

Fix a center point \(O\text{.}\) We write \(R_\theta\) to denote the rotation by angle \(\theta\) about the point \(O\text{.}\) Angle units can be revolutions or degrees or radians, whatever is most convenient. We observe the usual convention that positive values of \(\theta\) denote counterclockwise rotations and negative values of \(\theta\) denote clockwise rotations.

Reflections in the plane.

We write \(F_L\) to denote the reflection across the line \(L\text{.}\) This means that \(P'=F_L(P)\) if and only if \(\overline{PP'}\perp L\) and the distance from \(P\) to \(L\) is the same as the distance from \(P'\) to \(L\text{.}\)

Fact 1.1.5. Symmetries of the regular \(n\)-gon.

The symmetries of the regular \(n\)-gon is the set of all \(R_\theta,F_L\) for the following values of \(\theta,L\text{,}\) where \(O\) is the center of the \(n\)-gon.

\begin{gather} \theta = \frac{k}{n}\text{ revolutions}=k\frac{360}{n} \text{ degrees} = k\frac{2\pi}{n} \text{ radians}, k\in \Z\label{ngonrotations}\tag{1.1.1}\\ L=\overline{OP}, \; P \text{ any vertex}\label{ngonmirrorsymmsvert}\tag{1.1.2}\\ L=\overline{OM}, \; M \text{ the midpoint of any edge}\label{ngonmirrorsymmsmid}\tag{1.1.3} \end{gather}
This description is redundant. For example, one of the rotational symmetries of the square is by an angle of \(3/4\) revolutions or 270 degrees. This is the same as \(-1/4\) revolutions or \(-90\) degrees. In general, we have \(R_{\frac{k}{n}} = R_{-\frac{n-k}{n}}\text{.}\) There is also redundancy among the mirror symmetries. If \(n\) is even, then each vertex \(P\) has an opposite vertex P' such that \(\overline{OP}=\overline{OP'}\text{,}\) i.e., we have \(F_{\overline{OP}}=F_{\overline{OP'}}\text{.}\) If \(n\) is odd, then each vertex \(P\) has an opposite midpoint M such that \(\overline{OP}=\overline{OM}\text{,}\) i.e., we have \(F_{\overline{OP}}=F_{\overline{OM}}\text{.}\)

Given symmetries \(A,B\text{,}\) we write \(AB\) to denote the composition \(A\circ B\text{.}\) For example, for the symmetries of the equilateral triangle, with angles in degrees, and with \(L=\overline{OP}\) for some vertex \(P\text{,}\) we have \(R_{240}R_{120}=R_{0}\) and \(F_LR_{120}=R_{-120}F_L\text{.}\)

Definition 1.1.6. Dihedral group.

The dihedral group, denoted \(D_n\text{,}\) is the set of symmetries of the regular \(n\)-gon (see FactÂ 1.1.5 above) together with the binary operation of function composition.

Subsection 1.1.3 The norm 1 complex numbers

Definition 1.1.8. The circle group.

The circle group, denoted \(S^1\text{,}\) is the set

\begin{equation*} S^1=\{z\in \C\colon |z|=1\} \end{equation*}

of norm 1 complex numbers together with the binary operation \(S^1\times S^1\to S^2\) given by complex multiplication, that is, \((z,w)\to zw\text{.}\)

Subsection 1.1.4 Integers

Definition 1.1.10. The group of integers.
The set \(\Z\) of integers, together with the operation of addition, is called the group of integers. Similarly, the set \(\Z_n\) of integers modulo \(n\) (where \(n\) is some integer \(n\geq 1\)), together with the operation of addition modulo \(n\text{,}\) is called the group of integers mod \(n\).

Subsection 1.1.5 Invertible matrices

Let \(n\geq 1\) be an integer. We write \(GL(n,\R)\) to denote the set of \(n\times n\) invertible matrices with real entries.

Definition 1.1.11. The group of invertible matrices .

The set \(GL(n,\R)\text{,}\) together with the binary operation of matrix multiplication, is called the group of \(n\times n\) invertible matrices, or also the general linear group.

Subsection 1.1.6 Nonzero elements in a field

Let \(k\) be a field, such as the rational numbers \(\Q\text{,}\) the real numbers \(\R\text{,}\) or the complex numbers \(\C\text{.}\) We write \(k^\ast\) to denote the set of nonzero elements in \(k\text{.}\)

Definition 1.1.12. Group of nonzero elements in a field.
Let \(k\) be a field. The set \(k^\ast\text{,}\) together with the binary operation of multiplication, is called the group of nonzero elements in the field \(k\text{.}\)
