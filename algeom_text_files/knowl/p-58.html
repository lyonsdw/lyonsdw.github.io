Analogous to the way that the complex numbers \(\C\) can be realized as the set \({\mathcal M}_\C\) of \(2\times 2\) real matrices (see ExerciseÂ 1.1.7.12), the quaternions can be realized by a set of \(2\times 2\) complex matrices, as follows. Let \({\mathcal M}_\Quat\) denote the set of \(2\times 2\) complex matrices of the form \(\left[\begin{array}{cc} u \amp v\\ -v^\ast \amp u^\ast\end{array}\right]\text{.}\) Given a quaternion \(r=a+bi+cj+dk\text{,}\) let \(u,v\) be the complex numbers \(u=a+bi\) and \(v=c+di\text{,}\) and let \({M}(r)\) denote the \(2\times 2\) matrix in \({\mathcal M}_\Quat\) given by
\begin{equation*} {M}(r)=\left[\begin{array}{cc} u \amp v\\ -v^\ast \amp u^\ast\end{array}\right]. \end{equation*}
Conversely, given a matrix \(M\in {\mathcal M}_\Quat\text{,}\) with top left entry \(a+bi\) and top right entry \(c+di\text{,}\) let \(Q(M)\) denote the quaternion \(r=a+bi+cj+dk\text{.}\) It is clear that the mappings \(r\to M(r)\) and \(M\to Q(M)\) are inverses to one another, and establish a one-to-one correspondence \(\Quat\leftrightarrow {\mathcal M}_\Quat\text{.}\)
