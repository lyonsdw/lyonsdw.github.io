Given a complex number \(z\leftrightarrow (x,y)\text{,}\) let \((r,\theta)\) be polar coordinates for the point \((x,y)\) such that \(r\geq 0\) and \(\theta\) is measured in radians. The modulus or norm of \(z\text{,}\) denoted \(|z|\text{,}\) is defined to be the polar coordinate \(r=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\) and the argument of \(z\text{,}\) denoted \(\arg{z}\text{,}\) is the polar coordinate \(\theta\text{,}\) that is, the oriented angle made by the real vector \((x,y)\) with the positive real axis. In other words, \((|z|,\arg{z})\) are polar coordinates for the point \((x,y)\text{.}\) Here is a summary.
