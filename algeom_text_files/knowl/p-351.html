The input/output pairs \((1,0,0)\to (0,1,0)\text{,}\) \((0,0,1)\to (1,0,0)\) determine the first and third columns of an orthogonal matrix, namely \((0,1,0)^T,(1,0,0)^T\text{.}\) The middle column must then be \((0,0,\pm 1)^T\text{.}\) If, instead of an orthogonal matrix, you want to get the geometric information of an axis and angle that specifiy a rotation, you can do this using quaternions, as follows. One possible composition of rotations is \(R_{Y,\pi/2}\) followed by \(R_{X,\pi/2}\text{.}\) These rotations are realized by quaternions \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1+j)\text{,}\) \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1+i)\text{,}\) respectively. So the rotation we want is given by the quaternion

\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1+i)\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1+j) =\frac{1}{2}+\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\left(\frac{i+j+k}{\sqrt{3}}\right)=\cos \frac{\pi}{3} + \sin \frac{\pi}{3}u \end{equation*}

with \(u=\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(i+j+k)\text{.}\) By PropositionÂ 1.2.9, this is the rotation about \((1,1,1)\) by \(2\pi/3\) radians.
