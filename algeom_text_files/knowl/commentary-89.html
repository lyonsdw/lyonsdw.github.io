Instructor’s solution for Exercise 3.1.4.1.

  1. These are both 3-4-5 triangles. One way to map the first triangle to the second is the following sequence of transformations.
    1. shift left 6 units: \((x,y)\to (x-6,y)\)
    2. rotate 90 degrees (counterclockwise): \((x,y)\to (-y,x)\)
    3. shift down 1 unit: \((x,y)\to (x,y-1)\)
    4. reflect across the \(y\)-axis: \((x,y)\to (-x,y)\)
    Putting all this together, we get \((x,y)\to (y,x-7)\text{.}\) This is the Euclidean transformation \(v\to Rv + b\) with \(R=\twotwo{0}{1}{1}{0}\) and \(b=(0,-7)\text{.}\)
  2. The input/output pairs \((1,0,0)\to (0,1,0)\text{,}\) \((0,0,1)\to (1,0,0)\) determine the first and third columns of an orthogonal matrix, namely \((0,1,0)^T,(1,0,0)^T\text{.}\) The middle column must then be \((0,0,\pm 1)^T\text{.}\) The matrix with middle column \((0,0, 1)^T\) has determinant 1, so it is the rotation. You can find the axis of rotation by finding an eigenvector. It would then take more work to find the angle of rotation. Here is a way to use quaternions to determine the axis and angle. One possible composition of rotations is \(R_{Y,\pi/2}\) followed by \(R_{X,\pi/2}\text{.}\) These rotations are realized by quaternions \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1+j)\text{,}\) \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1+i)\text{,}\) respectively. So the rotation we want is given by the quaternion
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1+i)\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1+j) =\frac{1}{2}+\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\left(\frac{i+j+k}{\sqrt{3}}\right)=\cos \frac{\pi}{3} + \sin \frac{\pi}{3}u \end{equation*}
    with \(u=\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(i+j+k)\text{.}\) By Proposition 1.2.9, this is the rotation about \((1,1,1)\) by \(2\pi/3\) radians.
  3. We need a two-by-two invertible matrix \(T\) such that \(T(1,0)= \alpha(c,1)\text{,}\) \(T(0,1)=\beta(b,1)\) and \(T(1,1)= \gamma(a,1)\) for some \(\alpha,\beta,\gamma\text{.}\) The first two requirements are met by \(T=\twotwo{\alpha c}{\beta b}{\alpha}{\beta}\text{.}\) Solve the system \(T(1,1)=\gamma(a,1)\) leads to \((\alpha,\beta)\propto (a-b,c-a)\text{.}\) This leads to the projective transformation given by the matrix \(\twotwo{(a-b)c}{(c-a)b}{a-b}{c-a}\text{.}\)
  4. Let \(T_1,T_2\) be Euclidean transformations given by \(v\to R_1v+b_1\) and \(v\to R_2v+b_2\text{,}\) respectively. The composition
    \begin{equation*} T_2(T_1(v))=R_2(R_1v+b_1)+ b_2=R_2R_1v + (R_2b_1+b_2) \end{equation*}
    is indeed a Euclidean transformation. The identity element has \(R=\Id\) and \(b=(0,0)\text{.}\) The inverse of \(v\to Rv+b\) is given by \(v\to R^{-1}v - R^{-1}b\text{.}\)
  5. Let \(T\) be given by \(v\to Rv+b\text{.}\) We have
    \begin{equation*} d(T(P),T(Q))= |(RP+b)-(RQ+b)|=|R(P-Q)|=|P-Q|=d(P,Q) \end{equation*}
    (the penultimate equality holds because orthogonal transformations preserve vector norm).
