  1. The generators of \(\Z\) are \(\pm 1\text{.}\)

  2. The generators of \(\Z_8\) are \(1,3,5,7\)

  3. The generators of \(\Z_2\times \Z_3\) are \((1,1)\) and \((1,2)\)

  4. By direct checking, we find that none of the four elements of \(\Z_2\times \Z_2\) is a generator.

  5. We claim that \(\Z_m\times \Z_n\) is cyclic if and only if \(m,n\) are relatively prime. Here's the proof. Let \(L\) denote the least common multiple of \(m,n\text{,}\) so that \(L\leq mn\) and \(L=mn\) if and only if \(m,n\) are relatively prime. Next, observe that an element \((a,b)\) of \(\Z_m\times \Z_n\) is a generator if and only if every entry in the sequence

    \begin{equation*} (a,b),(2a,2b),(3a,3b),\ldots,(mna,mnb) \end{equation*}
    is distinct. If \(L\lt mn\text{,}\) then \((La,Lb)=(0,0)=(mna,mnb)\text{,}\) so \((a,b)\) is not a generator. This proves the "only if" direction. Conversely, suppose \(L=mn\) (that is, \(m,n\) are relatively prime). We claim that \((1,1)\) is a generator. If not, then there exist integers \(k,\ell\) in the range \(0\lt k,\ell\lt mn\) such that \(k\neq \ell\) and \((k,k)=(\ell,\ell)\text{.}\) Suppose, without loss of generality, that \(k\lt \ell\text{.}\) Then we have \((0,0)=(\ell-k,\ell-k)\text{,}\) so \(\ell-k\) is a integer number that is less than \(mn\text{,}\) and is divisible by both \(m\) and \(n\text{.}\) But this contradicts the assumption that the least common multiple of \(m,n\) is \(mn\text{.}\) This concludes the proof of the "if" direction, and the proof is complete.
