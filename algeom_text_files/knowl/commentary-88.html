Instructor's solution for Exercise 3.1.4.1.

  1. These are both 3-4-5 triangles. One way to map the first triangle to the second is the following sequence of transformations.

    1. shift left 6 units: \((x,y)\to (x-6,y)\)
    2. rotate 90 degrees (counterclockwise): \((x,y)\to (-y,x)\)
    3. shift down 1 unit: \((x,y)\to (x,y-1)\)
    4. reflect across the \(y\)-axis: \((x,y)\to (-x,y)\)

    Putting all this together, we get \((x,y)\to (y,x-7)\)

  2. One possible composition of rotations is \(R_{Y,\pi/2}\) followed by \(R_{X,\pi/2}\text{.}\) These rotations are realized by quaternions \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1+j)\text{,}\) \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1+i)\text{,}\) respectively. So the rotation we want is given by the quaternion

    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1+i)\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1+j) =\frac{1}{2}+\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\left(\frac{i+j+k}{\sqrt{3}}\right)=\cos \frac{\pi}{3} + \sin \frac{\pi}{3}u \end{equation*}

    with \(u=\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(i+j+k)\text{.}\) By Proposition 1.2.9, this is the rotation about \((1,1,1)\) by \(\pi/6\) radians.

  3. We need \(\alpha,\beta\) such that \((1,0)\to \alpha(c,1)\text{,}\) \((0,1)\to \beta(b,1)\) and \((1,1)\to \alpha(a,1)\text{.}\) Solve that system to get \((\alpha,\beta)\propto (a-b,c-a)\text{.}\) This leads to the projective transformation given by the matrix \(\twotwo{(a-b)c}{(c-a)b}{a-b}{c-a}\text{.}\)
  4. Let \(T_1,T_2\) be Euclidean transformations given by \(v\to R_1v+b_1\) and \(v\to R_2v+b_2\text{,}\) respectively. The composition
    \begin{equation*} T_2(T_1(v))=R_2(R_1v+b_1)+ b_2=R_2R_1v + (R_2b_1+b_2) \end{equation*}
    is indeed a Euclidean transformation. The identity element has \(R=\Id\) and \(b=(0,0)\text{.}\) The inverse of \(v\to Rv+b\) is given by \(v\to R^{-1}v - R^{-1}b\text{.}\)
  5. Let \(T\) be given by \(v\to Rv+b\text{.}\) We have
    \begin{equation*} d(T(P),T(Q))= |(RP+b)-(RQ+b)|=|R(P-Q|=|P-Q|=d(P,Q) \end{equation*}
    (the penultimate equality holds because orthogonal transformations preserve vector norm).
