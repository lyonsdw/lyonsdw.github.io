  1. First, observe that \(C(a)\) is not empty because \(e\in C(a)\text{.}\) Let \(g,h\) be elements of \(C(a)\text{.}\) We have
    \begin{equation*} (gh)(a)=g(ha)=g(ah)=(ga)h=(ag)h=a(gh) \end{equation*}
    so \(gh\) is in \(C(a)\text{.}\) Multiplying \(ag=ga\) on the right and left by \(g^{-1}\text{,}\) we have \(g^{-1}a=ag^{-1}\text{,}\) so \(g^{-1}\) is in \(C(a)\text{.}\) We conclude that \(C(a)\) is a subgroup of \(G\) by the 2-step subgroup test.
  2. You can give an argument just like for the previous part, with the quantifier "for all \(a\)". Alternatively, you can prove that the intersection of subgroups is a subgroup (this is "obvious"). Then simply note that
    \begin{equation*} Z(G) = \bigcap_{a\in G}C(a). \end{equation*}
