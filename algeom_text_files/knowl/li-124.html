Item a.
Clearly, \(r\) cannot be 1, and the statement is true for \(r=2\text{.}\) Now suppose the statement holds for \(k\lt r\) and let \(e=\tau_1\cdot \tau_r\) be given. The four possibilities for \(\tau_{r-1}\tau_r\) are the following, where \(a,b,c,d\) are distinct.
\begin{equation*} (ab)(ab), (ac)(ab), (bc)(ab), (cd)(ab) \end{equation*}
These products can be rewritten, respectively, as
\begin{equation*} e, (ab)(bc), (ac)(cb), (ab)(cd) \end{equation*}
In the first case, we have \(e=\tau_1\cdots \tau_{r-2}\text{,}\) so \(r-2\) must be even by the inductive hypothesis, so we are done. In the other three cases, we have an expression where the rightmost occurrence of the symbol \(a\) is in position \(r-1\text{.}\) Continuing this process, we continue to rewrite the product of transpositions until the number of transpositions drops by 2. This must happen, because otherwise the rightmost occurrence of the symbol \(a\) would be in the first transposition, but then the product would not be the identity because it would not fix \(a\text{.}\)
