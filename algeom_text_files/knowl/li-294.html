Item b.

The input/output pairs \((1,0,0)\to (0,1,0)\text{,}\) \((0,0,1)\to (1,0,0)\) determine the first and third columns of an orthogonal matrix, namely \((0,1,0)^T,(1,0,0)^T\text{.}\) The middle column must then be \((0,0,\pm 1)^T\text{.}\) The matrix with middle column \((0,0, 1)^T\) has determinant 1, so it is the rotation. You can find the axis of rotation by finding an eigenvector. It would then take more work to find the angle of rotation. Here is a way to use quaternions to determine the axis and angle. One possible composition of rotations is \(R_{Y,\pi/2}\) followed by \(R_{X,\pi/2}\text{.}\) These rotations are realized by quaternions \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1+j)\text{,}\) \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1+i)\text{,}\) respectively. So the rotation we want is given by the quaternion
\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1+i)\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1+j) =\frac{1}{2}+\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\left(\frac{i+j+k}{\sqrt{3}}\right)=\cos \frac{\pi}{3} + \sin \frac{\pi}{3}u \end{equation*}
with \(u=\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(i+j+k)\text{.}\) By PropositionÂ 1.2.9, this is the rotation about \((1,1,1)\) by \(2\pi/3\) radians.
