Exercise 7. Cyclic permutations.

Let \(k,n\) be positive integers with \(k\leq n\text{,}\) and let \(A=\{a_1,a_2,\ldots,a_k\}\) be a set of \(k\) distinct elements in \(\{1,2,\ldots,n\}\text{.}\) We write \((a_1a_2\cdots a_k)\) to denote the permutation \(\kappa\) in \(S_n\) (see DefinitionÂ 2.1.1) given by the assignments
\begin{equation*} a_1\stackrel{\kappa}{\to} a_2\stackrel{\kappa}{\to} a_3\stackrel{\kappa}{\to} \cdots\stackrel{\kappa}{\to} a_k\stackrel{\kappa} {\to} a_1 \end{equation*}
and \(\kappa(j)=j\) for \(j\not\in A\text{.}\) If \(k=1\text{,}\) the permutation \((a_1)\) is the identity permutation. A permutation of the form \(\kappa=(a_1a_2\cdots a_k)\) is called a \(k\)-cycle. For example, the element \(\kappa=[1,4,2,3]=(2\;4\;3)\) is a 3-cycle in \(S_4\) because \(\kappa\) acts on the set \(A=\{2,3,4\}\) by
\begin{equation*} 2\to 4\to 3\to 2 \end{equation*}
and \(\kappa\) acts on \(A^c=\{1\}\) as the identity. Note that cycle notation is not unique. For example, in \(S_4\) we have \((2\;4\;3)=(4\;3\;2)=(3\;2\;4)\text{.}\) A permutation is called cyclic if it is a \(k\)-cycle for some \(k\text{.}\) A 2-cycle is called a transposition.
  1. Find all of the cyclic permutations in \(S_3\text{.}\) Find their inverses.
  2. Find all of the cyclic permutations in \(S_4\text{.}\)
