Notice that \(SU(2)\) is the subset \({\mathcal M}_\Quat\) (see Proposition 1.2.1) of matrices with determinant 1. We know \({\mathcal M}_\Quat\) is closed under matrix multiplication, and determinant is multiplicative (that is, \(\det(AB)=\det A\det B\)), so \(SU(2)\) is closed under multiplication. It is easy to check that \(SU(2)\) is a group whose identity element is the identity matrix, and with inverse given by \(\twotwo{a}{b}{-b^\ast}{a^\ast}^{-1}=\twotwo{a\ast}{-b}{b^\ast}{a}\text{.}\) Alternatively, the fact that \(SU(2)\) is a group follows from the fact that it is the set of matrices with determinant 1 that preserve the complex inner product on \(\C^2\text{.}\) It is straightforward to check that \(M\) is a homomorphism and is one-to-one and onto (\(M^{-1}\) is an appropriate restriction of the map \(Q\) given in the paragraph that precedes Proposition 1.2.1).
