  1. Let \({\mathcal E}, {\mathcal O}\) denote the set of even, or odd, respectively, elements of \(S_n\text{.}\) Let \(\tau\) be a transposition (\(\tau=(12)\) will do fine). Notice that \(L_\tau\colon S_n\to \S_n\) given by \(L_\tau(\sigma)=\tau\sigma\) is a bijective map (its inverse is itself) and takes \({\mathcal E}\) to \({\mathcal O}\text{,}\) and vice-versa. Thus it must be the case that \(|{\mathcal E}|=|{\mathcal O}|\text{.}\)

  2. It is clear that the product of two even permutations is an even permutation. If \(\sigma=\tau_1\cdots \tau_r\) is a product of transpositions, then \(\sigma^{-1}=\tau_r\cdots \tau_1\text{,}\) so the inverse of an even permutation is also an even permutation. The set \(A_n\) is not empty because the identity is an even permutation. We conclude that \(A_n\) is a subgroup of \(S_n\) by the 2-step subgroup test.
