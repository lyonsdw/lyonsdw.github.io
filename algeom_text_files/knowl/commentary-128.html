Instructor's solution for Checkpoint 3.4.2.

Notice that \(SU(2)\) is the subset \({\mathcal M}_\Quat\) (see Proposition 1.2.1) of matrices with determinant 1. We know \({\mathcal M}_\Quat\) is closed under matrix multiplication, and determinant is multiplicative (that is, \(\det(AB)=\det A\det B\)), so \(SU(2)\) is closed under multiplication. The identity matrix is the identity element of \(SU(2)\) and the inverse operation is given by \(\twotwo{a}{b}{-b^\ast}{a^\ast}^{-1}=\twotwo{a\ast}{-b}{b^\ast}{a}\text{.}\) Alternatively, the fact that \(SU(2)\) is a group follows from the fact that it is the set of matrices with determinant 1 that preserve the complex inner product on \(\C^2\text{.}\) That the map \(M\colon U(\H)\to SU(2)\) is a homomorphism is a consequence of the definition of quaternion multiplication (1.2.5), namely, \(rs=M^{-1}(M(r)M(s))\) for quaternions \(r,s\text{.}\) Applying \(M\) to both sides, we have \(M(rs)=M(r)M(s)\text{.}\)
