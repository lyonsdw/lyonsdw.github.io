We have \((a,b,c)\stackrel{s}{\to} \frac{a+bi}{1-c}\) and \((a,-b,-c)\stackrel{s}{\to} \frac{a-bi}{1+c}\) so the question is whether \(\frac{a+bi}{1-c}\stackrel{\text{inversion}}{\to} \frac{a-bi}{1+c}\text{.}\) The answer is yes, as confirmed by this derivation.

\begin{equation*} \frac{a+bi}{1-c}\stackrel{\text{inversion}}{\to}\frac{1-c}{a+bi}=\frac{(1-c)(a-bi)}{a^2+b^2} = \frac{(1-c)(a-bi)}{1-c^2} = \frac{(1-c)(a-bi)}{(1-c)(1+c)} = \frac{a-bi}{1+c} \end{equation*}
