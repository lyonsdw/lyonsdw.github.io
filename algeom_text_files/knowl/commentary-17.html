We have \((x,y)\stackrel{s}{\to} \frac{x}{1-y}\) and \((x,-y)\stackrel{s}{\to} \frac{x}{1+y}\text{,}\) so the question is whether \(\frac{x}{1-y}\stackrel{\text{inversion}}{\to} \frac{x}{1+y}\text{.}\) The answer is yes, as confirmed by this derivation.

\begin{equation*} \frac{x}{1-y}\stackrel{\text{inversion}}{\to}\frac{1-y}{x}=\frac{(1-y)x}{x^2} = \frac{(1-y)x}{1-y^2} = \frac{(1-y)x}{(1-y)(1+y)} = \frac{x}{1+y} \end{equation*}
