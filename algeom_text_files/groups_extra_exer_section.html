Section 1.6 Additional exercises

Exercises 1.6.1 Exercises

1.

Let \(G\) be a finite group with identity \(e\text{.}\)

  1. Show that the number of elements \(g\) of \(G\) such that \(g^3=e\) is odd.
  2. Show that the number of elements \(g\) of \(G\) such that \(g^2\neq e\) is even.
2. The group of units in \(\Z_n\).

Let \(U_n\) denote the group of elements in \(Z_n\) that have multiplicative inverses, that is,

\begin{equation*} U_n = \{x\in \Z_n\colon \exists y, xy=1\pmod{n}\}. \end{equation*}
  1. Show that \(x\) is in \(U_n\) if and only if \(x\) is relatively prime to \(n\text{.}\) [Comment: The function \(n\to |U_n|\text{,}\) important in number theory, is called Euler's phi function, written \(\phi(n)=|U_n|\text{.}\)]
  2. Show that \(U_n\) with the binary operation of multiplication mod \(n\) is an Abelian group. [Comment: The group \(U_n\) is called the the group of (multiplicative) units in \(\Z_n\text{.}\)]
  3. Show that \(U_n\) is isomorphic to \(\Aut(\Z_n)\) via \(x\to [a\to ax]\text{.}\)
3. Fermat's Little Theorem.

For every integer \(x\) and every prime \(p\text{,}\) we have \(x^p = x \pmod{p}\text{.}\) Hint

First, reduce \(x\) mod \(p\text{,}\) that is, write \(x=qp+r\) with \(0\leq r\leq p-1\text{.}\) Now consider two cases. The case \(r=0\) is trivial. If \(r\neq 0\text{,}\) apply the fact \(r^{|G|}=e\) (see Exercise 1.3.2.9) to the group \(G=U_p\text{.}\)

4. Second Isomorphism Theorem.

Let \(H,J\) be subgroups of \(G\) with \(J\) normal in \(G\text{.}\) Then \(HJ=\{hj\colon h\in H, j\in J\}\) is a subgroup of \(G\text{,}\) \(H\cap J\) is a normal subgroup of \(H\text{,}\) and \(HJ/J\approx H/(H\cap J)\text{.}\)

5. Third Isomorphism Theorem.

Let \(H,J\) be normal subgroups of \(G\) with \(H\subseteq J\text{.}\) Then \(J/H\) is a normal subgroup of \(G/H\text{,}\) and \((G/H)/(J/H)\approx G/J\text{.}\)

6. Semidirect product.

Let \(K,H\) be groups, and let \(\phi\colon H\to \Aut(K)\) be a homomorphism. The semidirect product, denoted \(K\times_{\phi} H\text{,}\) or \(K\rtimes H\) if \(\phi\) is understood, is the set consisting of all pairs \((k,h)\) with \(k\in K\text{,}\) \(h\in H\) 1  with the group multiplication operation \(\ast\) given by

\begin{equation*} (k_1,h_1)\ast (k_1,h_2) = (k_1\phi(h_1)(k_2),h_1h_2). \end{equation*}

Two examples demonstrate why this is a useful construction. The dihedral group \(D_n\) is (isomorhpic to) the semidirect product \(C_n\rtimes C_2\text{,}\) where \(C_n\) is the cyclic group generated by the rotation \(R_{1/n}\) (rotation by \(1/n\) of a revolution) and \(C_2\) is the two-element group generated by any reflection \(R_L\) in \(D_n\text{.}\) The map \(\phi\colon C_2 \to \Aut(C_n)\) is given by \(F_L \to [R_{\theta} \to R_{-\theta}]\text{.}\) The Euclidean group of congruence transformations of the plane is (isomorphic to) the group \(\R^2\rtimes O(2)\text{,}\) where \((\R^2,+)\) is the additive group of \(2\times 1\) column vectors with real entries, and \(O(2)\) is the group of \(2\times 2\) real orthogonal matrices. The map \(\phi\colon O(2)\to \Aut(\R^2)\) is given by \(g\to [v\to gv]\text{,}\) that is to say, the natural action of \(O(2)\) on \(\R^2\text{.}\) [The Euclidean group element \((v,g)\) acts on the point \(x\in\R^2\) by \(x\to gx+v\text{.}\)]

  1. Do all the necessary details to show that \(K\rtimes H\) is indeed a group.
  2. Show that \(D_n\approx C_n\rtimes C_2\text{,}\) as described above.
  3. Show that the following requirement holds for the Eucliden group action. We have
    \begin{equation*} [(v_1,g_1)(v_2,g_2)]x = (v_1,g_1)[(v_2,g_2)x], \end{equation*}
    for all \(v_1,v_2,x\in \R^2\) and \(g_1,g_2\in O(2)\text{.}\)
  4. Suppose that \(\phi\colon H\to \Aut(K)\) is the trivial homomorphism (that is, \(\phi(h)\) is the identity homomorphism on \(K\text{,}\) for all \(h\in H\)). Show that \(K\times_{\phi} H\approx K\times H\) in this case.
The notation \(K\times H\) is understood to be the direct product group, so we do not write use the Cartesian product notation to describe semidirect product, to avoid confusion, even though the underlying set for the direct product and the semidirect product are in fact the same Cartesian product \(K\times H\text{.}\)
7. Exact sequences, short exact sequences, and splittings.

A sequence of groups and homomorphisms

\begin{equation*} \cdots \underset{f_{i-2}}{\longrightarrow} G_{i-1} \underset{f_{i-1}}{\longrightarrow} G_{i} \underset{f_{i}}{\longrightarrow} G_{i+1} \underset{f_{i+1}}{\longrightarrow}\cdots \end{equation*}

is exact if \(\ker(f_{i})=\im(f_{i-1})\) for all \(i\text{.}\) A short exact sequence of groups and homomorphisms is an exact sequence of the form

\begin{equation*} 1\to K\to G\to H\to 1 \end{equation*}

where \(1\) denotes the trivial group. A short exact sequence

\begin{equation} 1{\to} K \underset{i}{\longrightarrow} G \underset{\pi}{\longrightarrow} H\to 1\label{SES}\tag{1.6.1} \end{equation}

is said to \(split\) if there is a group homomorphism \(s\colon H\to G\) such that \(\pi\circ s\) is the identity homomorphism on \(H\text{.}\)

  1. Show that for a short exact sequence of the form (1.6.1), that the homomorphism \(i\) is one-to-one, the homomorphism \(\pi\) is onto, and \(G/K\approx H\text{.}\)
  2. Suppose that (1.6.1) splits, with splitting \(s\colon H\to G\text{.}\) Show that \(\phi\colon H\to \Aut(K)\) given by
    \begin{equation*} \phi(h)(k) = i^{-1}\left(f(h)i(k)f(h)^{-1}\right) \end{equation*}
    is defined 2  and is a homomorphism.
  3. Show that the short exact sequence (1.6.1) splits, with splitting \(s\text{,}\) if and only if \(G\approx K\times_{\phi} H\text{,}\) where \(\phi\) is described in the previous part above.
There is slight abuse of notation with the symbols "\(i^{-1}\)". The homomorphism \(i\) is not invertible (because it is not onto), but \(i\) is invertible on its image. So one of the things to check is that \(f(h)i(k)f(h)^{-1}\) is indeed in the image of \(i\) so that applying \(i^{-1}\) makes sense.
8. Group action on functions on a \(G\)-space.

Suppose that a group \(G\) acts on a set \(X\text{.}\) Let \({\mathcal F}(X,Y)\) denote the set of functions

\begin{equation*} {\mathcal F}(X,Y) = \{f\colon X\to Y\} \end{equation*}

from \(X\) to some set \(Y\text{.}\) Show that the formula

\begin{equation*} (g\cdot \alpha)(x) = \alpha(g^{-1}\cdot x) \end{equation*}

defines an action of \(G\) on \({\mathcal F}(X,Y)\text{,}\) where \(g\in G\text{,}\) \(\alpha \in {\mathcal F}(X,Y)\text{,}\) and \(x\in X\text{.}\)
