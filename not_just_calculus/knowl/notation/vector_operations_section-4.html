An ordered pair is sometimes called a point in \(\R^2\text{,}\) and sometimes called a vector, depending on context.
 1 
Many texts make a distinction by using the notation \((a,b)\) for a point and \(\langle a,b\rangle\) for a vector. In these notes, we do not observe this convention.
The ordered pair \((a,b)\) may be denoted using a capital letter, say \(P=(a,b)\text{,}\) when we think of \((a,b)\) as a point in the plane, or it may be denoted by a lower case bold letter, say \({\mathbf x}=(a,b)\text{,}\) or by a lower case letter with an arrow, for example \(\vec{x}=(a,b)\text{,}\) when we think of \((a,b)\) as a vector. Another way to denote a vector \((a,b)\) is an expression of the form \(\overrightarrow{QR}\text{,}\) where \(Q,R\) are points related by \(R=Q+(a,b)\text{.}\) The ordered pair \((0,0)\) is called the origin or the zero vector, depending on whether we are thinking of \((0,0)\) as a point or as a vector, respectively. The numbers \(a,b\) are called the Cartesian coordinates or the rectangular coordinates of the ordered pair \((a,b)\text{.}\) The vectors \({\mathbf e}_1 = (1,0)\) and \({\mathbf e}_2= (0,1)\) are called the standard basis vectors . We have the following expressions for \({\mathbf x}=(a,b)\) in terms of the standard basis vectors.
\begin{equation} {\mathbf x} = a{\mathbf e}_1 + b{\mathbf e}_2= ({\mathbf e}_1\cdot {\mathbf x}) {\mathbf e}_1 + ({\mathbf e}_2\cdot {\mathbf x}) {\mathbf e}_2.\tag{2.1.4} \end{equation}
