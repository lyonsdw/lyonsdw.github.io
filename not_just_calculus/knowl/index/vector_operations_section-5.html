The ordered pair \((0,0)\) is called the origin or the zero vector, depending on whether we are thinking of \((0,0)\) as a point or as a vector, respectively. It is common practice to use the capital letter \(O\) to denote the origin and to use the symbols \(\mathbf{0}\) or \(\vec{0}\) to denote the zero vector.
\begin{align*} O \amp = (0,0) \amp\amp \text{(the origin point)}\\ \mathbf{0} =\vec{0} \amp = (0,0) \amp\amp \text{(the zero vector)} \end{align*}
Beware overloaded notation! Given a point \(P=(a,b)\text{,}\) the vector \(\mathbf{x}=\overrightarrow{OP}\) is also represented by the ordered pair \((a,b)\text{.}\) In other words, we have \(P=(a,b)\) and \(\mathbf{x}=(a,b)\text{,}\) but \(P\neq \mathbf{x}\) (because a point and a vector are two different types of objects, they cannot be equal). The takeaway message is that you must always be clear whether you mean a point or a vector when you write an ordered pair.
