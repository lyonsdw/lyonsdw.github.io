Let \(f\colon S\to T\) be a function. A consequence of Proposition 1.1.5, Proposition 1.1.6, and Proposition 1.1.7, is that the following conditions on \(f\) are equivalent.
  1. The function \(f\) is bijective.
  2. There exists a function \(g\colon T\to S\) such that \(g\of f = \One_S\) and \(f\of g = \One_T\text{.}\)
The statement that these conditions are equivalent means if \(f\) satisfies condition 1, then \(f\) satisfies condition 2, and vice-versa. If \(f=(S,T,G)\) satisfies these conditions, then \(f\) is called invertible, and the function \(g=(T,S,G'=\{(t,s)\colon (s,t)\in G\})\) is called the inverse of \(f\text{,}\) denoted \(g=f^{-1}\) .
