Given a relation \(R\subseteq S\times T\text{,}\) the inverse relation to \(R\), denoted \(R^{-1}\text{,}\) is the relation from \(T\) to \(S\) given by
\begin{equation*} R^{-1}=\{(t,s)\colon (s,t)\in R\}\subseteq T\times S. \end{equation*}
In general, the inverse relation \(f^{-1}\) of a function \(f\) is not a function. However, if \(f\colon S\to T\) is bijective, then every element \(t\) in \(T\) is the right entry of exactly one element in \(f\text{,}\) so every \(t\) in \(T\) is the left entry of exactly one element in \(f^{-1}\text{,}\) so \(f^{-1}\colon T\to S\) is a function.
